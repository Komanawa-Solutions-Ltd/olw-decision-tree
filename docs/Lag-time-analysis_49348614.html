OLW Monitoring Design Framework : Lag time analysis

Created by Zeb Etheridge, last modified on Mar 30, 2021

Background and purpose

The purpose of this page is to provide guidance on how to define and classify lag times to guide DST inputs

Knowledge of lag time between implementation of mitigation actions and the full effect of these actions on water quality at a receptor or compliance monitoring point is a critical requirement for water quality monitoring design. Upstream monitoring, located close to the area of mitigation, may be required for receptors with a long lag time. Knowledge of lag time required to interpret mitigation efficacy from monitoring results.

Lag time and land use change information is also critical for mitigation efficacy analysis in catchments where significant changes in nitrogen leaching rates have occurred within the equilibration period

Definitions

Term

Definition

Lag time:

Period between a change in the nitrogen leaching rate and realisation of the effects at a monitoring location.

Equilibration period

Period between a change in the nitrogen leaching rate and realisation of the full effects at a monitoring location, i.e. to achieve steady state conditions, with no further trend over time

Breakthrough time

The time taken for a change in the nitrogen leaching rate in the catchment to become detectable at a monitoring location (assuming no background noise)

Mean residence time

The average age of water in a water sample. See discussion below.

Age distribution

Water travels through aquifers and into surface waters via a mixture of faster and slower pathways. The age distribution describes the mixture of ages in a water sample.

Mean residence time (MRT)

The average age of water in a water sample.

Effective Lag Time (ELT)

We used the term Effective Lag Time (ELT) to describe the time taken for 90% of the effects of a change in catchment nitrogen inputs to be realised at a given monitoring location. Use of the ELT is preferable to the equilibrium period, which could become impractical due to long tail age distributions. It is also preferable to the mean residence time, represents an unspecified proportion (e.g. 50% for a gaussian distribution) of the nitrate concentration change.

Method

The process for determination of the Effective Lag Time (ELT) for a given monitoring site is as follows:

  1. Collect and analyse age tracer samples, taking specialist advise on sample number and timing as required.

  2. Translate age tracer concentrations into water age using an appropriate age distribution model, taking specialist advice as required.

  3. Use age distribution model to determine the 90th percentile age, i.e. the ELT
