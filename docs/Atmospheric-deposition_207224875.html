OLW Monitoring Design Framework : Atmospheric deposition

Created by Simon Linke, last modified on Jan 18, 2021

Water quality and nutrient input in most lakes is driven by nutrient loadings - mostly from Surface inflow, but also Groundwater inputs. However, the proportion of the nutrient load from atmospheric sources can be substantial in lakes situated in relatively pristine catchments. The main nutrients from atmospheric sources are nitrate (NO3) and ammonia/ammonium (NH3/NH4). Sources of atmospheric deposition are derived from the combustion of burning of fossil fuels and from intensive farming practices - the latter being the main driver in New Zealand.

The relative contribution from atmospheric deposition to nutrient loads is higher in lakes with very small catchments and in oligotrophic lakes where nutrient inputs from the catchment are low. For instance, in Lake Taupo, atmospheric deposition accounts for about 20% of both N and of P loads (Vant and Gibbs 2006). Even higher ratios were found in Lake Rotorua (Vant and Gibbs 2006).

Unfortunately, there are few recent studies estimating the average contributions of atmospheric deposition to lake nutrient loads and measurement is complex. Overall, Verburg et al. (2018) estimate that with recent agricultural intensification in many parts of the country, the proportion of lake nutrient budgets contributed by atmospheric deposition has probably increased.

References:

Verburg, Piet, Marc Schallenberg, Sandy Elliott, and Chris G. McBride. "Nutrient budgets in lakes." In Lake Restoration Handbook, pp. 129-163. Springer, Cham, 2018.

Vant, B., and M. Gibbs. "Nitrogen and Phosphorus in Taupo Rainfall. Environment Waikato Technical Report 2006/46." (2006).
