OLW Monitoring Design Framework : Case Study- Riparian Management DST

Created by Olivier Ausseil, last modified by Amy Whitehead on Mar 30, 2021

We applied the riparian management DST to a case study catchment to identify the likely effectiveness of three different riparian mitigation options across the landscape for four water quality variables. This assessment followed the methodology detailed here. In addition, we undertook an analytical power analysis on each reach in the catchment to identify how likely it would be that we would be able to detect the expected reduction if mitigation was effective. This analysis provides a demonstration of the riparian management DST and shows how it could be used to prioritise reaches in the catchment for placing monitoring sites. All analyses were conducted using R.

Our application of the riparian management DST focused on a catchment in Taranaki, with three main sub-catchments (Figure 1). This catchment was chosen because we had access to underlying physical and water quality data to allow the models to be run. In each mitigation scenario, we applied the mitigation to the central sub-catchment and left the remaining two sub-catchments unmitigated. We assumed that the water quality conditions in the unmitigated sub-catchments were constant over time.

Figure 1. Map of the case study catchment, with the mitigated sub-catchment indicated in yellow.

Predicting the effectiveness of mitigation

We modelled the likely effectiveness of three riparian mitigation options: 1) fencing, 2) a grass buffer and 3) a mixed buffer on four water quality variables (total suspended solids, shade, water temperature and periphyton cover). The efficacy of each mitigation option for each water quality variable was obtained from the Mitigation Options table. For the purposes of this case study, we made a number of assumptions because the underlying data were not readily available. While these assumptions are likely to influence the results, they were considered to be appropriate for this demonstration of the riparian mitigation DST approach.

  • Hillslope length (100m), soil clay content (15%) and bank height (1m) were set to constant values throughout the catchment.

  • The source of sediment load to each instream reach was assumed to be 50% bank erosion and 50% runoff .

  • The buffer width, vegetation height, planting setback and time to maturity were assumed to be constant within a mitigation scenario.

    • Fencing for stock exclusion: buffer width (0m), vegetation height (0m), planting setback (0m), time to maturity (1 year).

    • Grass buffer: buffer width (5m), vegetation height (1m), planting setback (0.5m), time to maturity (1 year).

    • Mixed buffer: buffer width (10m), vegetation height (5m), planting setback (0.5m), time to maturity (10 years).

  • The initial state of each water quality variable was obtained from existing models of water quality that have been predicted onto the digital river network (Table 1).

Figure 2 shows the expected state of the catchment for each water quality variable under the three mitigation scenarios, while Figure 3 highlights the expected change in state compared to the initial starting conditions. What is evident from these figures is that the fencing mitigation scenario is only likely to be effective at reducing TSS, with no impact on shade, water temperature or periphyton cover. In contrast, the grass and mixed buffer mitigation scenarios are expected to result in improvements in all four water quality variables, although greater improvements are likely with a mixed buffer for shade and periphyton in some parts of the catchment. It is important to note that we haven’t considered the effects of the mitigation scenarios on nutrients, and the associated links with periphyton growth, in this demonstration.

Only the central sub-catchment is mitigated in each scenario (Figure 1), while the no mitigation scenario represents the initial state of the catchment.

Figure 3. The expected reduction in state for four water quality variables (columns) when three mitigation scenarios (rows) reach maturity. Blue tones represent a greater change in state, while grey reaches indicate no change.

Predicting the power to detect a change in state

The second part of this analysis identifies the parts of the catchment where we might be most likely to detect a statistically significant change in water quality state. We used a statistical power analysis at the reach-scale, with reaches predicted to have high power prioritised as sites where monitoring is likely to be most effective for detecting change. For the purposes of this demonstration, we used the expected reduction in each water quality variable at each reach, with the standard deviation calculated as the reach reduction multiplied by a random number between 0.8 - 1.2. The results presented below assume a Type I error of 0.05. For each scenario, we calculated the power to detect the expected reduction at specified time periods after the mitigation has matured (i.e., become fully established; fencing & grass buffers - 1 year, mixed buffer - 10 years), assuming a monthly sampling regime. We have assumed that the mitigation is fully effective at mitigating each of the water quality variables at this time and have not considered lag effects.

Figure 4. The power available to detect the expected reduction in state (Figure 3) for four water quality variables (columns) five years after three mitigation scenarios (rows) have reached maturity. Yellower tones indicate a higher power, while grey reaches indicate no expected change in state and, therefore, no power to detect a change.

Figure 4 highlights that the power to detect the expected reductions in water quality state varies by water quality variable and mitigation type, as well as spatially throughout the catchment. Increased power was associated with greater expected magnitudes of reduction. For example, we will have greater power to detect reductions in TSS because those reductions are expected to be greater. While there appears to be little difference in power between the grass and mixed buffer scenarios, it is apparent that we would be unlikely to detect an effect of these mitigation actions on water temperature and periphyton after five years of the mitigations being fully effective (i.e., six years after implementation of the grass buffer and 15 years after implementation of the mixed buffer). This analysis also highlights that the location of reaches within the stream network with the highest power also varies by water quality variable, again linked to the expected reduction in state. While the power to detect an statistically significant reduction increases with the number of samples taken (Figure 5), we may still be unlikely to detect a reduction in some variables even after 10 years of monitoring.

The power available to detect the expected reduction in state (Figure 3) for four water quality variables (columns) at three time steps after three mitigation scenarios (rows) have reached maturity. Yellower tones indicate a higher power, while grey reaches indicate no expected change in state and, therefore, no power to detect a change. The analysis assumes a monthly sampling regime.

Designing a monitoring network

The third aspect of this demonstration aims to identify parts of the catchment that may be most effective for undertaking monitoring to detect improvements in water quality state in relation to the mitigation actions. Here we have taken the results of the power analyses for each of the water quality variables and calculated summary statistics (minimum, mean, maximum) across each time period to identify where the power is greatest (Figure 6). High values of maximum power identify reaches where we would be likely to detect the expected reduction in at least one of the monitored water quality variables, while the high values of minimum power can be used to identify reaches where a change is likely to be detected for all four water quality variables. The minimum and maximum values for the fencing scenario are the same as we only expect to see a reduction in TSS.

As expected, the power to detect a reduction increases with the length of the monitoring period and is greater where the expected reductions across all variables are greater. The maximum power plots indicate that we will likely detect a significant reduction in any one of the measured variables anywhere within the mitigated sub-catchment five years after mitigation for the grass or mixed buffers. In contrast, ten years of sampling would be required for the fencing mitigation scenario and reductions would be more likely to be detected in lower order reaches in the upper catchment. Note however that the fencing results are based solely on reductions in TSS as fencing for stock exclusion is not considered effective for reducing shade, temperature or periphyton (Figure 2 & Figure 3). In all cases, establishing a monitoring site right at the bottom of the catchment is likely to be ineffective at detecting reductions associated with the mitigations as there is very low power. This effect is largely due to the 'diluting' effects of the unmitigated sub-catchments.

Figure 6. The maximum, mean and minimum power available to detect the expected reductions across all water quality variables for three mitigation scenarios (rows) at three time steps (columns). Yellower tones indicate a higher power, while grey reaches indicate no expected change in state and, therefore, no power to detect a change. The analysis assumes a monthly sampling regime.
